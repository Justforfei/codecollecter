The Science to Building Teams

Posted September 28, 2015

Every successful business has a great team working behind the scenes. Getting a great group of people collaborating together doesn’t just happen by chance. There is a wealth of studies and experiments to testify that there is indeed a science to building teams. We’re going to share some knowledge that we’ve acquired over the years about how to create great teams.

Stars Are Dependent On The Team

A Harvard study published in 2006 revealed that star performers were highly influenced by the support of their team. The study was based on heart surgeons and patient mortality. When a heart surgeon operates on a patient with the help of the surgeon’s regular medical team, the rate of patient mortality significantly decreased. This highlights the importance of the team rather than individual effort.

Team Building Exercises Actually Work!

Awkward small talk and uncomfortable interactions at work events are often associated with team building exercises. The Small Group Research Journal Paper produced an article called Does Team Building Work, (April 2009, vol. 40 no. 2 181-222). One hundred and three studies over the course of 57 years all came to the same conclusion – team building has measurable, positive effects on team performance. A great point that was raised in the study was that  teams need to be able to communicate with each other about non work related issues in order for them to feel truly comfortable about discussing work issues. Team building exercises will improve natural communication between teams. The best team building exercises are those that feel normal and social rather than forced – Things like sport and dining work well.

TED Talks Marshmallows

In 2006, Peter Skillman, introduced the world to the Marshmallow Challenge in his TED Talk. In 2010, Tom Wujec later spoke about it in his TED Talk too.

The marshmallow challenge is an experiment where teams of four are formed. They are given 20 sticks of spaghetti, one yard of string, one yard of tape, and one marshmallow. The objective is for each team to build the tallest free standing structure in 18 minutes with the condition being that the marshmallow has to be on the top of the building.

This task seems simple, but it is actually quite difficult as it forces collaboration and requires teams to work together quickly. This task forces people to collaborate, innovate, and create.

Surprising lessons about teamwork and group dynamics emerge from these tests. Statistically, the weakest groups who compete in these tests are business graduates. Apart from architects, the most successful group that take part in these studies are children from the age of 4–6 years. This highlights a number of issues.

Business graduates, as well as many other adults, strive to be in charge, too many strong voices and different opinions will lead to a poor plan- that combined with a lack of experience in professional team activities. The ability to communicate and work together is something that comes naturally to children. Egos and power trips aren’t synonymous with childhood. These attributes negate successful communication and teamwork. Andrew Carnegie, leader of the American Steel Industrial Revolution, once said that “Teamwork is the fuel that allows common people to attain uncommon results.” This is true for the children in the studies. They were the least educated in the studies, however they were the best versed group in teamwork.

The marshmallow challenge proves that the science of teams isn’t about collecting a group of the smartest people in the world, it is about inspiring communication within groups. Groups who can communicate effectively with each other will always be the best teams.

Personality Tests

In the 1970’s,  Paul Costa and Robert McCrae came to the conclusion that there are five main personality traits: The Big Five.

They are:

  1. Openness / Closed minded
  2. Conscientiousness / Disorganized
  3. Extraverted / Introverted
  4. Agreeable / Disagreeable
  5. Emotional stability / Neuroticism

(Try out this test here to see where you score).

Openness: Those who score high for this trait tend to enjoy adventure and be open to new experiences.

Conscientiousness: High scorers for conscientiousness are generally organized and dependable.

Extraversion: Those who are high on this scale draw their energy from being around others, so they tend to be more sociable.

Agreeableness: High scorers for this trait are often trusting, helpful, and compassionate.

Emotional stability: People with high scores for this trait are usually confident and don’t tend to worry often (this may be tested as neuroticism, in which case high scorers would be prone to worrying and anxiety).

Every team needs a mix of people (i.e., the leader, the dissenter, the communicator, etc.). If you know the personalities of your employees then you will be able to create a balanced and great team. Personality tests delve into the psychology and the science of people, or more specifically the study of the mind and how it influences behavior. Jody Porowski, CEO and Founder of Avelist, writes “If you believe that people are the core of business then you have to agree that the science of people plays an important role in the success of a company. From marketing a product to engaging in team discussions to selling your vision to investors, you’ll do a better job if you understand the people that you’re interacting with”.

Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is one of the most sought after skill in management and leadership positions. Emotional intelligence refers to a persons ability to understand and work well with people. People with high levels of emotional intelligence are best equipped to build teams. Personality tests help us to identify and understand how individuals behave. If you figure out people’s natural talents then you will be able to create the best teams. Groups are made up of individuals. In order to create great teams, you have to understand that individuals and their personalities comprise these teams. Many large companies make new hires take personality or competency tests in some stage of the recruitment. These test a person’s behavioral traits and categorize candidates into groups of preferred and not preferred personality groups. Companies who don’t prescribe personality tests to new hires still take personality into high consideration when hiring new staff. They will call it finding the right fit to match company culture.

Summing Up

Great teams don’t just happen they are made, not in a lab, but nonetheless it can’t be discounted that there is a science to building teams. With a name like Teamwork.com, we like to pride ourselves on our knowledge of teamwork and teams. But we are always trying to learn more, so if you have any tips or questions about group collaboration, comment below!
