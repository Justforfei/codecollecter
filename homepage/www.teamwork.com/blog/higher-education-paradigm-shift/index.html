The Higher Education Paradigm is Shifting

Posted August 17, 2015

Guest post by Rebel Smith, Ed.D.

Learning is a lifelong process and online courses make that possible. The higher education paradigm is shifting to accommodate the complex, modern student.

Let’s start with a story. Everyone loves a good story. A long, long time ago (the 80’s) in a land far, far away (okay, not so far away, Oklahoma), a young girl (me) began her collegiate journey. She had chosen a private, liberal arts university, and she had a plan – to be finished in four years. Then, life happened. She transferred, as a very high percentage of students do, and met her prince charming. They married before she finished her degree. This was definitely not in the plan. Neither was the fact that he was in the military. He got orders far, far away (this time for real, Alaska). The young college student received permission to obtain her remaining hours off-campus. When she finally finished her degree, she had attended six universities. This is not an unusual story.

If our heroine time-traveled to today, she, and others like her, would have access to options that would help her earn a degree faster.

I am a firm believer in higher education; it is the great equalizer. An education allows first-generation students from low-income families to change their lives, as well as the lives of their children. It pulls people out of poverty. And today’s higher education is much different from what was available just a few years ago. Today it is all about access. Students can choose between private or public, a large state school or a smaller regional university. And they can choose to study on-campus or online. There is something for everyone.

Do I think that online learning is replacing brick-and-mortar institutions? Absolutely not. I loved my time on-campus; I was very involved in my sorority and various other clubs. It is a common belief among higher education administrators that the 18- to 22-year-old, traditional college student belongs on campus. This is a time and place for significant learning, and not just what occurs in the classroom.  

But what about non-traditional students: those who are married, have children, must work full- or part-time? Not very long ago, these students were often unable to finish their degrees.  They dropped out or “stopped out,” which is stopping and starting as finances and other circumstances allow. Graduation rates for this group are low, but they don’t have to be.

Students can choose from a variety of online options. Many brick-and-mortar institutions offer online classes and degree programs. Many of the same faculty members who teach on-campus also teach online. Students can start their degrees on-campus and then finish online when circumstances require that they do so. And students who were never able to start in the first place, can start now.

The traditional 16-week semesters or five to eight-week sessions are available, and many offer flexible start dates. Some institutions allow students to take their first class at no charge, giving students the chance to try online learning risk-free. And most online programs charge no out-of-state tuition. Even international students are able to attend. No student visa is needed; they remain in their countries and study online.

Online students are eligible for financial aid and receive many of the same services as do those who study on-campus: tutoring, academic advising, technical support, etc.

Another new trend in higher education is competency-based degree plans. In competency-based learning, the course is divided into competency modules. One student may take two weeks to complete the module or master the competency, while another may take four or six. This allows students to work through at their own pace and they are able to use their past knowledge (competencies) to their advantage. This is appealing to older students who may have many years of experience in a specific area. If they are able to prove their competency, they can then move forward quickly and not spend a set amount of time (the same as everyone else) learning a skill or competency when it’s not needed. Combine this with online and there is incentive for many who have not finished their degree to now do so.

To summarize, online programs are available from private and public institutions, large and small. Class sessions are five to 16 weeks, and there are often multiple start-dates offered. Online programs allow non-traditional students and others who do not live near an institution and are unable to move the opportunity to obtain a degree. They are high-quality and affordable. Competency-based programs are available, as are those that offer credit for past experience (professional and academic). Today, it would be easier for our young heroine (me) to finish her degree, no matter where life takes her.

To this point, our discussion has centered around undergraduate online programs. I want to shift gears and talk briefly about graduate programs. Students are now able to complete master’s, specialist, and even doctoral degrees online. The target population for these programs are working professionals who are unable to attend traditional on-campus, daytime classes.

If you are looking for a master’s or even a doctorate, you can find one online to meet your needs. This allows you to keep your current position and salary and do your coursework when it is convenient for you. A student who has dreamed of attending an Ivy League institution like Harvard or Yale, or their state school that is located several hours away, can now do so.

Whether students are looking for a bachelor’s degree or a master’s, when studying online they tend to make their choices differently. Students who plan to study on-campus will visit several institutions and then choose the one with the best fit. It may be based on the physical beauty of the campus, the athletic facilities and teams, the classroom technology, or the residence halls. When students are choosing an online program, they tend to choose the program first, for instance, a bachelor’s in general business. They will then research schools that offer this program. Factors that tend to influence this decision include cost and length of program. So, rather than identify their dream school, this group of students acts more like the savvy shopper, comparing products and choosing the best one for them. This has become quite a competitive area, as more and more reputable institutions enter the arena of online learning. This is good for the student. If you are looking for a master’s in educational leadership with classes offered in six-week sessions, you can find several to choose from.

I am happy to see higher education offering new ways to meet the needs of more students. We are not all alike, but we can all benefit from continuing our education.

Dr. Rebel Smith is Director of Recruitment and Enrollment Management for Online and Distance Education Programs at the University of Arkansas in Fayetteville, Arkansas.
